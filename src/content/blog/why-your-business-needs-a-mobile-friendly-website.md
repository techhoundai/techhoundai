At least once a month, a potential client tells us they need a mobile app for their business. When we ask what the app would do, the answer is usually some version of: let customers see our services, read about us, and get in touch.

That's not an app. That's a website. And if your website works properly on a phone, you don't need an app at all.

There's a persistent myth in the small business world that having a mobile app makes you look more professional or more technologically advanced. The reality is that a bad mobile app is worse than no app at all, and most small businesses don't have the kind of use case that justifies building and maintaining one.

Let's talk about what you probably actually need and when a real app does make sense.

## How People Actually Use Their Phones

When someone hears about your business and pulls out their phone to look you up, they're not going to the App Store. They're opening their browser, typing your name or your service into Google, and tapping the first relevant result.

That result is your website. If it looks good on their phone, loads quickly, and makes it easy to find your phone number or fill out a contact form, you just won a customer. If it loads slowly, has tiny text they need to pinch to read, or has buttons so small they tap the wrong one, you just lost one.

Over 60% of all web traffic now comes from mobile devices. For local businesses, that number is even higher. The mobile experience on your website isn't a nice-to-have feature. It's the primary way most people will interact with your business online.

## What "Mobile-Friendly" Actually Means

A mobile-friendly website (the technical term is "responsive") automatically adjusts its layout based on the screen size. On a desktop, you might see three columns of content side by side. On a phone, those same three columns stack vertically so everything is readable without zooming.

But it goes beyond just rearranging content. A genuinely mobile-friendly site considers:

**Touch targets.** Buttons and links need to be large enough to tap accurately with a finger. If your navigation menu has links packed so tightly that users keep hitting the wrong one, that's a mobile experience problem.

**Loading speed.** Mobile connections are often slower than desktop connections, especially outside of major cities. Large images, heavy scripts, and unnecessary code all slow things down. A mobile-friendly site is also a lightweight site.

**Readability.** Text should be large enough to read without zooming. Line lengths should be appropriate for a small screen. Nobody should have to scroll horizontally to read a sentence.

**Simplified navigation.** The navigation structure that works on a 27-inch monitor doesn't work on a 6-inch phone. Mobile sites typically use a hamburger menu (the three horizontal lines) or other condensed navigation patterns.

**Phone number clickability.** This one is specific to businesses that want phone calls, which is most local businesses. Your phone number should be a tap-to-call link, not just text. Making someone memorize or manually type your number when they're already on their phone is leaving money on the table.

## When You Actually Need a Mobile App

There are legitimate reasons to build a mobile app, but they're more specific than most people think.

**Offline functionality.** If your users need to access your product without an internet connection, an app is the way to go. Think of a field service tool where technicians need to pull up job details in a basement with no signal.

**Hardware access.** If your product needs deep integration with the phone's camera, GPS, Bluetooth, sensors, or other hardware, a native app provides better access than a website.

**Repeated daily use.** If your customers interact with your product multiple times per day, every day, an app can provide a smoother experience. This is why banking apps, ride-sharing apps, and messaging apps work well as native applications. The frequency of use justifies the real estate on someone's home screen.

**Push notifications.** If real-time notifications are a core part of your product, a native app handles them more reliably than web-based alternatives.

**Complex interactions.** If your product involves sophisticated gestures, real-time graphics, or performance-intensive features, a native app will generally perform better than a mobile website.

If your business doesn't fit any of those categories, you probably don't need an app. What you need is a website that doesn't fall apart on a phone.

## The Cost Difference

Here's the practical reality that seals the deal for most small businesses. Building a mobile app costs significantly more than building a responsive website. We're talking $15,000 to $50,000 or more for a custom app, compared to a few thousand dollars for a well-built responsive site.

And that's just the build cost. Apps need to be maintained for two separate platforms (iOS and Android), updated when Apple and Google release new operating system versions, and submitted to app stores that can reject them for any number of reasons. There's an ongoing maintenance burden that doesn't exist with a website.

A responsive website, on the other hand, works on every device with a browser. Build it once, maintain it once, and it works everywhere.

## How to Test Your Current Site

Pull out your phone and visit your own website. Go through it like a potential customer would:

1. Can you read everything without zooming?
2. Can you tap buttons and links easily?
3. Does the page load in under three seconds?
4. Can you find your phone number and tap to call?
5. Can you fill out the contact form without wanting to throw your phone?

If the answer to any of those is no, your mobile experience needs work. And if you're losing even a small percentage of mobile visitors because of a poor experience, the cost of fixing it pays for itself quickly.

## What to Do About It

If your current website doesn't work well on mobile, you have two options. You can retrofit your existing site to be responsive, or you can build a new one from scratch. Which path makes sense depends on how your current site was built and how much work it would take to fix it.

If you're considering building a new site anyway, make sure mobile is a primary consideration from day one, not something that gets tacked on at the end. The best approach is to design for mobile first and scale up to desktop, not the other way around.
