One of the first questions we get from new clients is some version of: "I need a website, but I have no idea what kind." And honestly, it's a fair question. The web development world throws around a lot of jargon, and most of it isn't helpful if you're just trying to get your business online.

So let's cut through the noise. There are really only a few categories of websites that matter for most businesses, and once you understand what each one does, the choice becomes pretty straightforward.

## The Simple Marketing Site

This is the most common type of website for small businesses, and for good reason. A marketing site is essentially your digital storefront. It tells people who you are, what you do, where you're located, and how to get in touch. Think of it like a brochure that's available 24/7.

These sites are typically five to ten pages. You'll have a homepage, an about page, a services page, a contact page, and maybe a blog or portfolio. They load fast, they're easy to maintain, and they [cost less](/blog/why-software-doesnt-have-to-cost-what-it-used-to/) than more complex options.

If your main goal is to show up when someone searches for your type of business and give them enough confidence to call you, a marketing site is probably all you need.

## The E-Commerce Site

If you sell physical or digital products, you need an online store. E-commerce sites include product listings, a shopping cart, payment processing, and usually some form of inventory management.

The complexity here depends on how many products you sell and what kind of checkout experience you want. A local bakery selling a dozen items has very different needs than a clothing brand with hundreds of SKUs and multiple size variants.

One thing to keep in mind: e-commerce sites require more ongoing maintenance. Products change, prices update, inventory fluctuates. You need a platform that makes those updates easy, or a team that handles them for you.

## The Web Application

This is where things get more involved. A web application is a website that does something, not just displays information. Think of tools like project management software, customer portals, booking systems, or internal dashboards.

If your business has a process that currently lives in spreadsheets or email chains and you want to turn it into a proper tool, you're talking about a web application. These are custom-built for your specific needs and require more planning, development time, and budget.

Not every business needs one, but for those that do, it can be transformational. We've seen companies cut hours of manual work per week by moving a single process into a custom web app.

## The Landing Page

Landing pages are single-page websites designed to do one thing: convert a visitor into a lead or customer. They're focused, minimal, and built around a single call to action.

You might use a landing page for a specific marketing campaign, a product launch, or a special offer. They work well alongside a full website as targeted entry points for paid advertising.

The key with a landing page is clarity. Every element on the page should push the visitor toward one decision. If you're trying to do too many things on a single page, it stops being a landing page and starts being a confusing homepage.

## How to Decide

Here's a quick way to think about it:

- **"I just need people to find me and contact me."** Marketing site.
- **"I sell products and need to take payments online."** E-commerce site.
- **"I have a business process I want to turn into a tool."** Web application.
- **"I'm running a campaign and need a focused page."** Landing page.

Most businesses start with a marketing site and add on from there as they grow. That's a perfectly fine approach. You don't need to build everything at once. If you're not sure whether you need a static or dynamic build, we break that down in our guide to [static vs. dynamic websites](/blog/static-vs-dynamic-websites-which-one-does-your-business-need/).

## A Note on "Website Builders"

You might be wondering about tools like Wix, Squarespace, or WordPress.com. They're fine for getting something up quickly if you're comfortable doing it yourself. But they come with tradeoffs: limited customization, slower performance, ongoing subscription fees that add up over time, and templates that make your site look like everyone else's.

If you're serious about your online presence and want something that actually reflects your brand, a custom-built site is worth the investment. It doesn't have to cost a fortune, either. The tooling has gotten a lot better in recent years, and that has brought prices down significantly.
