If you've been paying any attention to the tech world over the past year, you've probably heard the term "AI agent" thrown around. It's become one of those buzzy phrases that means different things to different people, which makes it hard to figure out whether it's something your business should actually care about.

Let's break it down in plain terms.

## What an AI Agent Actually Is

An AI agent is a piece of software that can take actions on its own to accomplish a goal. That's the key distinction between an AI agent and a regular chatbot or AI assistant. A chatbot answers questions. An agent does things.

Think of it like this: if you ask ChatGPT to write an email, it will write the email and show it to you. An AI agent, on the other hand, could write the email, send it, check for a reply, follow up if there's no response, and update your CRM to reflect what happened. All without you doing anything after the initial instruction.

The "agent" part means it has some degree of autonomy. You give it a goal, and it figures out the steps to get there.

## How They Work

Under the hood, an AI agent combines a language model (the same kind of technology behind ChatGPT or Claude) with the ability to use tools. Those tools might include sending emails, querying a database, updating a spreadsheet, calling an API, or interacting with software your business already uses.

The agent receives an instruction, breaks it into steps, executes each step using the appropriate tool, and checks whether the result meets the goal. If something goes wrong, a well-built agent can adjust its approach and try again.

The important thing to understand is that the agent's capabilities are defined by the tools it has access to. An agent with access to your email and calendar can schedule meetings and send follow-ups. An agent with access to your inventory system can track stock levels and place reorders. The AI provides the reasoning; the tools provide the ability to act.

## Where AI Agents Make Sense for Businesses

Not every business process is a good candidate for an AI agent. The best use cases share a few characteristics: they're repetitive, they follow a general pattern (even if the details change each time), and they currently take up a meaningful amount of someone's time. (For more on the common pitfalls when automating, see [What Small Businesses Get Wrong About Automation](/blog/what-small-businesses-get-wrong-about-automation).)

Here are some concrete examples:

**Customer intake and qualification.** An agent can handle initial inquiries, ask qualifying questions, gather relevant information, and route leads to the right person on your team. Instead of someone spending 30 minutes on every new inquiry, they spend 5 minutes reviewing what the agent already collected.

**Appointment scheduling.** An agent can coordinate availability, propose times, handle rescheduling, and send reminders. It works around the clock, so customers can book at 11pm on a Sunday without waiting for your office to open Monday morning.

**Data entry and reconciliation.** If your team spends hours moving data between systems, copying information from emails into your CRM, or reconciling records between platforms, that's a strong candidate for an agent.

**Internal knowledge base.** Larger teams often have a lot of institutional knowledge scattered across documents, wikis, email threads, and people's heads. An AI agent trained on that information can answer employee questions instantly, reducing the time people spend searching for answers or interrupting colleagues.

**Report generation.** Rather than having someone pull data from three different sources and compile a weekly report manually, an agent can do it automatically and deliver it to your inbox every Monday morning.

## What They're Not Good At (Yet)

AI agents have real limitations, and it's important to be honest about them.

They're not great at tasks that require genuine creativity or subjective judgment. They can draft a marketing email, but they shouldn't be making strategic decisions about your brand positioning. They can summarize data, but interpreting what that data means for your specific business context still requires a human.

They also struggle with edge cases that fall outside their training. A well-built agent handles the 90% case reliably. The other 10% needs human oversight. This is why the best implementations always include a way for the agent to escalate to a person when it encounters something it isn't sure about.

And they can make mistakes. Just like any AI system, agents can occasionally produce incorrect outputs or take unexpected actions. This is why monitoring and guardrails are essential, especially when the agent has the ability to take real actions like sending emails or modifying records.

## Build vs. Buy

There are two broad approaches to getting an AI agent for your business. You can use an off-the-shelf product, or you can have one built specifically for your needs.

Off-the-shelf solutions work well for common use cases like customer support chat or appointment scheduling. They're faster to deploy and usually cheaper upfront. The tradeoff is that they're less flexible and might not integrate smoothly with the specific tools your business uses.

Custom-built agents take longer to develop and cost more initially, but they're tailored to your exact processes and integrate directly with your existing systems. ## Getting Started

If you're curious about whether an AI agent could help your business, the best first step is to make a list of tasks that eat up your team's time but don't require much creative thinking. Look for patterns: things that happen the same way over and over, involve moving information between systems, or follow a predictable set of steps.

That list is your starting point. From there, you can evaluate whether a tool already exists for the job or whether something custom would be a better fit.
